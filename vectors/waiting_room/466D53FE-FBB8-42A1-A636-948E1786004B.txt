Tone: collaborative

Summary:
In a collaborative effort, the UK Government announced new measures to tackle stalking, including statutory 'right to know' guidance to reveal the identity of online stalkers to victims, multi-agency statutory guidance, and enhanced stalking protection orders.
The proposed policies address significant gaps identified in a report citing the need for improvement in police responses and aim to bolster the legislative framework and victim support systems.
These measures received cross-party support, highlighting a unified parliamentary commitment to better protect victims of stalking and enhance accountability for perpetrators.

Key Points:
Jess Phillips (Labour, Birmingham Yardley):
    Jess Phillips addressed the House, stating the Government's intention to improve responses to stalking through several measures, including the introduction of multi-agency statutory guidance and reviewing existing legislation. She shared accounts of stalking victims who felt inadequately protected and emphasised the seriousness of stalking as a crime affecting mental health. The Government will launch a 'right to know' initiative for online stalking victims and allow courts to impose stalking protection orders independently. Phillips also recognised the contribution of activist Nicola Thorp and pledged collaboration with victim support organisation..
    
Supported by: Matt Vickers (Conservative, Stockton West), Jo White (Labour, Bassetlaw), Rachel Taylor (Labour, North Warwickshire and Bedworth), Julian Lewis (Conservative, New Forest East)

Matt Vickers (Conservative, Stockton West):
    Matt Vickers expressed support for the Government's new measures but urged further efforts to combat violence against women and girls. He appreciated Nicola Thorp's courage in sharing her storey and acknowledged previous government actions, such as increased sentencing for stalkers and measures within the Online Safety Act. Vickers also inquired about the metrics for measuring progress towards halving the rate of violence against women and girls.
    Context: In response to Jess Phillips' statement on new stalking measures.
Supported by: Jess Phillips (Labour, Birmingham Yardley)

Jo White (Labour, Bassetlaw):
    Jo White raised concerns about the vulnerability created by blocking on social media, which might exacerbate stalking. She asked about collaboration with the Department for Science, Innovation and Technology to address these issues.
    Context: Following Jess Phillips' statement on tackling stalking.

Lisa Smart (Liberal Democrat, Hazel Grove):
    Lisa Smart welcomed the Government's announcement but highlighted her personal challenges with how the police handle stalking under harassment law. She emphasised the importance of not having a one-size-fits-all approach and inquired about better training for police and 999 call handlers. Smart also asked about overcoming court backlogs to ensure timely justice for victims.
    Context: In response to Jess Phillips' statement on improved measures against stalking.

Rachel Taylor (Labour, North Warwickshire and Bedworth):
    Rachel Taylor expressed appreciation for the minister's statement and emphasised the local police in North Warwickshire and Bedworth supporting the new measures. She shared plans to accompany local police to directly address women and girls' experiences of violence and stalking.
    
Supported by: Jess Phillips (Labour, Birmingham Yardley)

Jess Phillips (Labour, Birmingham Yardley):
    Jess Phillips affirmed the ongoing commitment to halving violence against women and girls and outlined how success would be measured. She detailed the upcoming metrics from the Office for National Statistics and stressed that this mission is supported at the highest levels of government. Phillips acknowledged prior efforts but suggested that street-level impact might have been limited.
    Context: In response to Matt Vickers' inquiry about progress metrics for reducing violence against women and girls.
Supported by: Matt Vickers (Conservative, Stockton West)

Jess Phillips (Labour, Birmingham Yardley):
    Jessica Phillips highlighted technological capabilities in preventing stalking and emphasised collaboration between tech companies and police to ensure safety online. She reiterated that online and offline illegal activities should be treated equally, reaffirming actions to enhance stalking protection orders and engaging law enforcement effectively.
    Context: In response to Jo White's questions regarding technology and data protection in stalking cases.

Julian Lewis (Conservative, New Forest East):
    Sir Julian Lewis inquired about the challenges of identifying electronic stalkers, questioning whether issues stem more from internet companies or police adherence to privacy concerns.
    Context: Discussion during the debate on tackling stalking regarding technology and data privacy.
Supported by: Jess Phillips (Labour, Birmingham Yardley)

Peter Swallow (Labour, Bracknell):
    Peter Swallow emphasised the unsettling experience of stalking online and offline and asked how the 'right to know' guidance would empower and protect victims, helping them to regain control over their situations.
    

Jess Brown-Fuller (Liberal Democrat, Chichester):
    Jess Brown-Fuller commended the emerging practises in Cheshire constabulary that could become standardised across police forces, questioning how consistent practise can be ensured across different regions to prevent a postcode lottery in victim support.
    Context: Following discussion on police response to stalking.

Jonathan Davies (Labour, Mid Derbyshire):
    Jonathan Davies supported the new measures and highlighted the need to learn from failures in stalking cases where mistakes have led to tragic outcomes. He suggested using such cases as learning opportunities to enhance police response. Davies called for stronger action against tech companies failing to address stalking.
    Context: Inquiry about systemic failures in handling stalking cases and tech company's role during the debate.
