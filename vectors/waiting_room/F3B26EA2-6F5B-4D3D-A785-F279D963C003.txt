Tone: contentious

Summary:
In a highly charged debate, UK politicians clashed over the proposed increase in employer National Insurance contributions (NICs) to 15% and the reduction of the per-employee threshold to £5,000, with significant concern about the economic impacts on businesses and employment.
Conservative MPs, led by Mel Stride, voiced strong opposition, citing potential job losses and increased costs for charities and healthcare providers, while Labour defended the policy as necessary to address fiscal challenges and invest in public services like the NHS.
Notable tension came from accusations of broken promises and disagreements on economic strategy, highlighting the contentious nature of the debate with no clear cross-party consensus or practical resolution achieved.

Topics:
Economy, Business, and Infrastructure:
  Fiscal Policy and Public Spending, Employment and Labour Markets, Small Business Support

Key Points:
Mel Stride (Conservative, Central Devon):
    Increasing employers’ National Insurance contributions to 15% and lowering the per-employee threshold to £5,000 will increase costs for businesses, lower wages, lead to job losses, raise retailer costs by £2.3 billion, create a £1.4 billion annual bill for charities, and increase childcare costs. The government has not published a full impact assessment, and current economic conditions show low growth and confidence.
    
Opposed by: Toby Perkins (Labour, Chesterfield)

Mel Stride (Conservative, Central Devon):
    The Labour party misled the Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR), which estimated a £22 billion fiscal pressure. However, the OBR found this to be less than half, indicating the Treasury often manages fiscal pressures. Business confidence is low, and the national insurance increase is against a previous manifesto promise.
    Context: Rebuttal to Mr Toby Perkins' earlier points about economic management and OBR estimates.
Opposed by: Toby Perkins (Labour, Chesterfield)

Mel Stride (Conservative, Central Devon):
    The Conservative manifesto promised not to raise national insurance; however, they did so citing COVID-19’s economic impact. The OBR predicts 50,000 fewer jobs due to budget measures, harming sectors with low wages and young workers, contrary to Labour's claims of growth and low inflation.
    Context: In response to questions about consistency and the logic of raising taxes.
Opposed by: Paul Waugh (Labour (Co-op), Rochdale)

Mel Stride (Conservative, Central Devon):
    13 years of Conservative Government showed immense employment creation, but Labour’s proposals risk harming vulnerable sectors and increasing inflation, despite claims that they will make people better off.
    Context: Discussion of historical economic impacts and Labour's manifesto promises.

Opposed by: Jeevun Sandher (Labour, Loughborough)
