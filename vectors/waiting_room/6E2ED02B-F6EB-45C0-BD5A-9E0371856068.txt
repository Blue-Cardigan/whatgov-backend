Key Points:
Lord Offord of Garvel (Conservative, Life peer):
    Lord Offord of Garvel, Conservative, proposed Amendment 21 to include nuclear energy in Great British Energy's objectives. He emphasised the UK's historical leadership in nuclear energy and its current decline, stressing the importance of nuclear power for reliable, low-carbon electricity and energy security. He highlighted global commitments to nuclear energy and underscored the UK's vulnerability owing to imported energy. Lord Offord advocated for expanding nuclear energy production to stabilise energy prices and ensure energy security, urging the government to prioritise nuclear energy in its strategy.
    
Supported by: Baroness Bloomfield of Hinton Waldrist (Conservative, Life peer), Lord Ashcombe (Conservative, Excepted Hereditary)
Opposed by: Lord Hunt of Kings Heath (Labour, Life peer)
Keywords: nuclear energy, reliable electricity, energy security, net zero, energy strategy

Baroness Morris of Bolton (Conservative, Life peer):
    Baroness Morris of Bolton, Conservative, intervened to note that if Amendment 21 is agreed, Amendment 24 cannot be called due to pre-emption.
    
Keywords: amendment pre-emption, parliamentary procedure

Baroness Bloomfield of Hinton Waldrist (Conservative, Life peer):
    Baroness Bloomfield of Hinton Waldrist, Conservative, supported Amendment 21, emphasising the necessity of nuclear energy for achieving energy security and net-zero goals. She advocated for tangible government commitment to nuclear energy, urging public investment and private sector incentives, and highlighted the role of Great British Nuclear in coordinating nuclear development.
    Context: Supporting Lord Offord of Garvel's proposal on nuclear energy.
Keywords: public investment, nuclear energy, energy security, private sector incentives, Great British Nuclear

Lord Ashcombe (Conservative, Excepted Hereditary):
    Lord Ashcombe, Conservative, briefly supported Lord Offord, adding that nuclear energy is critical for consistent electricity generation, complementing renewable energy sources and enabling hydrogen production.
    Context: Supporting Lord Offord's emphasis on nuclear energy.
Keywords: nuclear energy, electricity generation, hydrogen production, renewable energy

Lord Hunt of Kings Heath (Labour, Life peer):
    Lord Hunt of Kings Heath, Labour, acknowledged the fundamental role of nuclear energy but stated that an amendment was unnecessary as nuclear power is already a governmental priority. He highlighted ongoing investments, such as Hinkley Point C and Sizewell C, and emphasised the need for skilled workers in the nuclear industry.
    Context: Responding to Lord Offord's Amendment 21 on nuclear energy.
Opposed by: Lord Offord of Garvel (Conservative, Life peer)
Keywords: nuclear industry, governmental priority, Hinkley Point C, Sizewell C, skilled workforce

Lord Offord of Garvel (Conservative, Life peer):
    Lord Offord of Garvel, Conservative, thanked Lord Hunt for supporting nuclear energy and urged clarity on GB Energy's nuclear involvement. He reflected on discussions concerning energy policy.
    Context: Dialogue following Lord Hunt's response to Amendment 21.
Keywords: GB Energy, nuclear involvement, energy policy clarity, dialogue

Lord Hunt of Kings Heath (Labour, Life peer):
    Lord Hunt of Kings Heath reconfirmed coordination between Great British Energy and Great British Nuclear, citing energy committee discussions.
    Context: Response to Lord Offord's emphasis on clarity.
Keywords: energy committee, GB Energy coordination, Great British Nuclear, energy policy

Lord Hamilton of Epsom (Conservative, Life peer):
    Lord Hamilton of Epsom, Conservative, questioned the high cost of a new power station in Somerset compared to a similar project in South Korea, expressing concerns about energy costs.
    Context: General critique on energy project costs.
Keywords: energy project costs, Somerset power station, international comparison

Lord Hunt of Kings Heath (Labour, Life peer):
    Lord Hunt of Kings Heath responded by explaining reasons for cost differences, including regulatory compliance requiring design changes. He noted learning improvements from Hinkley Point C to Sizewell C.
    Context: Response to Lord Hamilton's cost concerns.
Keywords: regulatory compliance, project cost differences, Hinkley Point C, Sizewell C

Lord Ashcombe (Conservative, Excepted Hereditary):
    Lord Ashcombe, Conservative, proposed Amendment 22 to include North Sea oil and gas exploration in Great British Energy's objectives to ensure energy security. He urged a pragmatic approach to achieving net-zero goals and highlighted the UK's energy reliance on gas imports, advocating for continued domestic production to secure energy supply and reduce emissions.
    
Keywords: North Sea oil and gas, energy security, net-zero goals, domestic production, energy supply

Lord Offord of Garvel (Conservative, Life peer):
    Lord Offord of Garvel supported Lord Ashcombe's Amendment 22, emphasising that focusing solely on renewables for energy security is unrealistic. He warned against dependency on imports prone to price fluctuations, advocating for sustained North Sea oil and gas production to protect the economy and energy supply. He criticised the current taxation on North Sea energy firms, suggesting it deters investment.
    Context: Supporting Lord Ashcombe’s focus on North Sea resources.
Keywords: renewable energy focus, import dependency, North Sea oil taxation, investment deterrence, economic protection

Lord Hamilton of Epsom (Conservative, Life peer):
    Lord Hamilton of Epsom expressed scepticism about net-zero goals, citing high costs and international non-compliance, especially from coal-dependent countries like China and India. He emphasised the continued necessity of oil and gas and warned against economic damage from banning North Sea exploration.
    Context: Expressing scepticism on net-zero strategies.
Keywords: net-zero scepticism, international compliance, oil and gas necessity, economic impact

Lord Hunt of Kings Heath (Labour, Life peer):
    Lord Hunt of Kings Heath highlighted global shifts towards renewable energy, including China's advancements. He acknowledged the need for an orderly transition using North Sea resources while committing to strategic climate goals. He stressed the opportunities and responsibilities linked to this transition strategy, responding to critiques on government plans.
    Context: Addressing concerns about energy transitions and strategies.
Keywords: renewable energy transition, orderly transition, global advancements, North Sea resources, climate goals

Lord Ashcombe (Conservative, Excepted Hereditary):
    Lord Ashcombe reiterated concerns about higher effective emissions from importing LNG versus domestic production and hinted at revisiting the issue in future debates.
    Context: Continuing discussion on Amendment 22 related to energy imports and emissions.
Keywords: LNG imports vs domestic production, effective emissions, future debate

Lord Offord of Garvel (Conservative, Life peer):
    Lord Offord of Garvel discussed financial provisions related to Great British Energy, emphasising the need for clarity and accountability in financial assistance. He proposed amendments to scrutinise the scope of financial powers granted to the Secretary of State and ensure financial integrity to protect taxpayers.
    
Supported by: Baroness Noakes (Conservative, Life peer), Lord Hamilton of Epsom (Conservative, Life peer)
Keywords: financial accountability, Great British Energy, taxpayer protection, Secretary of State powers

Baroness Noakes (Conservative, Life peer):
    Baroness Noakes, Conservative, sought clarification on certain financial assistance mechanisms in Clause 4 of the Great British Energy Bill, questioning how these provisions pertain to Great British Energy.
    Context: Seeking clarification on financial mechanisms.
Keywords: financial assistance, Clause 4, Great British Energy, mechanism clarification

Lord Hamilton of Epsom (Conservative, Life peer):
    Lord Hamilton of Epsom raised concerns about wasting taxpayer money on non-commercial energy projects under Great British Energy, highlighting potential losses if the government invests in unprofitable initiatives.
    Context: criticising potential waste of taxpayer funds in energy projects.
Keywords: taxpayer money, non-commercial projects, government investment, potential losses

Lord Cryer (Labour, Life peer):
    Lord Cryer, Labour, defended the financial framework for Great British Energy, explaining the necessity of set-up and operational funding provisions, structured within fiscal oversight guidelines.
    Context: Defending financial structure for Great British Energy against scrutiny.
Keywords: financial framework, operational funding, fiscal oversight, government budgeting

Lord Alton of Liverpool (Crossbench, Life peer):
    Lord Alton of Liverpool proposed Amendment 43 to prevent financial assistance to Great British Energy if there's credible evidence of modern slavery in the supply chain. He cited forced labor concerns, particularly in China's solar panel production, and the UK's reliance on it for energy targets. Alton emphasised the ethical obligation not to benefit from forced labor and suggested aligning UK policies with international standards against modern slavery.
    
Supported by: Earl Russell (Liberal Democrat, Excepted Hereditary)
Keywords: modern slavery, supply chain ethics, solar panel production, forced labor, international standards
