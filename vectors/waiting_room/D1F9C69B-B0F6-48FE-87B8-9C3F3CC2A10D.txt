Tone: collaborative

Summary:
The parliamentary debate, led by Debbie Abrahams, focused on amplifying disabled people's leadership and the removal of societal barriers that hinder their full societal participation.
Cross-party support was evident, with MPs from Labour, the DUP, and the Liberal Democrats urging action against disability discrimination, promoting employment, and enhancing accessibility measures in public services.
Ministerial commitments included enhancing jobcentre support for disabled individuals, establishing a disability employment panel, and progressing legislation to mandate disability pay gap reporting, marked by the collaborative tone of the proceedings.

Key Points:
Debbie Abrahams (Labour, Oldham East and Saddleworth):
    Debbie Abrahams emphasised the importance of the International Day of Persons with Disabilities, highlighting the role of disabled people in creating an inclusive future. She urged the government to involve disabled people actively in decision-making processes.
    

Jim Shannon (Democratic Unionist Party, Strangford):
    Jim Shannon commended Debbie Abrahams for her support of disabled people and emphasised the need for improved accessibility in public transport, including lifts and ramps for wheelchair users. He questioned if she agreed with these improvements.
    

Debbie Abrahams (Labour, Oldham East and Saddleworth):
    Debbie Abrahams agreed with Jim Shannon and planned to discuss the necessary challenges to improve accessibility and address hate crimes against disabled people, which have increased significantly since 2010.
    Context: Response to Jim Shannon's point on accessibility.
Supported by: Jim Shannon (Democratic Unionist Party, Strangford)

Steve Darling (Liberal Democrat, Torbay):
    Steve Darling raised the issue of floating bus stops and advocated for a government ban, mentioning the National Federation of the Blind's campaign. Debbie Abrahams acknowledged the importance and considered supporting the cause.
    

Debbie Abrahams (Labour, Oldham East and Saddleworth):
    Debbie Abrahams highlighted that nearly 16 million people in the UK have a disability, emphasising that despite existing policies signed by the UK under the UN convention on the rights of persons with disabilities, austerity measures have led to increased difficulties for disabled individuals.
    

Warinder Juss (Labour, Wolverhampton West):
    Warinder Juss highlighted the importance of recognising hidden disabilities like autism and depression, and suggested addressing unconscious bias, implying agreement with Debbie Abrahams' social model of disability approach.
    

Debbie Abrahams (Labour, Oldham East and Saddleworth):
    Debbie Abrahams acknowledged Warinder Juss' point about hidden disabilities and elaborated on poverty issues among families with disabled members. She criticised the government's handling of welfare reforms that adversely affected disabled people.
    Context: Response to Warinder Juss' point on hidden disabilities.
Supported by: Warinder Juss (Labour, Wolverhampton West)

Jonathan Davies (Labour, Mid Derbyshire):
    Jonathan Davies discussed the underrepresentation of deaf and hard of hearing women in service design, an issue he raised after meeting with the group 'Deaf-initely Women'. Debbie Abrahams acknowledged the problem of decimated services.
    

Debbie Abrahams (Labour, Oldham East and Saddleworth):
    Debbie Abrahams referred to a 2017 UN investigation that found the UK government guilty of systemic violations against disabled people’s rights, emphasising the need for a revised welfare system and accessibility improvements, including issues with public transport and education disparities.
    

Richard Baker (Labour, Glenrothes and Mid Fife):
    Richard Baker highlighted the low employment rate among disabled people in the UK, advocating for equal access to employment opportunities. Debbie Abrahams agreed, emphasising the 30% employment gap and the need for improved opportunities for disabled jobseekers.
    

Debbie Abrahams (Labour, Oldham East and Saddleworth):
    Debbie Abrahams stressed the importance of shining a light on employment disparities such as the disability pay gap, expressing optimism over new government commitments for disability pay gap reporting. She emphasised that financial support must be appropriately tuned without excessive application burdens.
    Context: Response to Richard Baker's employment equality concerns.
Supported by: Richard Baker (Labour, Glenrothes and Mid Fife)

Stephen Timms (Labour, East Ham):
    Sir Stephen Timms congratulated Debbie Abrahams for leading the debate and emphasised his department's commitment to enhancing disability inclusion by removing barriers and ensuring access in various life areas.
    

Samantha Niblett (Labour, South Derbyshire):
    Samantha Niblett shared the struggles of her nine-year-old constituent Alfred, who has cerebral palsy, and his family's fight for support. She requested a meeting between Alfred's family and the Minister to discuss necessary system changes.
    

Stephen Timms (Labour, East Ham):
    Sir Stephen Timms agreed to meet with Samantha Niblett and Alfred's family, outlining government efforts to support disabled people desiring work, highlighting the 'Get Britain Working' White Paper and upcoming independent reviews for better support at work.
    Context: Response to Samantha Niblett's concern about support for disabled children.
Supported by: Samantha Niblett (Labour, South Derbyshire)

Layla Moran (Liberal Democrat, Oxford West and Abingdon):
    Layla Moran mentioned a constituent named Keith, advocating for high-quality adult social care, and invited the Minister to Oxfordshire to discuss this. Sir Stephen Timms reiterated commitment to a national care service alongside the NHS plan.
    

Stephen Timms (Labour, East Ham):
    Sir Stephen Timms responded to Layla Moran, stating the government's commitment to breaking down barriers, including those in universal credit discouraging work among people with ill-health or disabilities.
    Context: Response to Layla Moran’s query on adult social care for people with disabilities.
Supported by: Layla Moran (Liberal Democrat, Oxford West and Abingdon)

Steve Darling (Liberal Democrat, Torbay):
    Steve Darling raised concerns about discrimination against disabled people and suggested they lack full legal protection compared to others with protected characteristics. Sir Stephen Timms highlighted forthcoming legislation that will support disabled people's rights explicitly.
    

Stephen Timms (Labour, East Ham):
    Sir Stephen Timms discussed the government's Race and Disability Equality Bill, which will mandate disability pay gap reporting and strengthen equal pay rights for disabled individuals. He also mentioned the accessibility advances seen at Google's Discovery Centre, emphasising ongoing government efforts.
    Context: Response to Steve Darling's concerns about legal protection for disabled people.
Supported by: Steve Darling (Liberal Democrat, Torbay)
