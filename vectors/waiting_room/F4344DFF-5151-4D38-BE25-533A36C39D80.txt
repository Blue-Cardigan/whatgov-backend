Summary:
Labour has proposed a permanent reduction in business rates for high street retailers, hospitality, and leisure properties with plans to implement these changes by 2026, funded by increasing taxes on properties valued over £500,000.
Constructive discussions highlighted challenges and support for small businesses, with calls for multinationals to contribute fairly, while reassurances were given about banking hub rollouts.
Minister James Murray defended the tax reform's potential against concerns from the DUP and urged Parliament's support to stabilise and invigorate high streets.

Topics:
Economy, Business, and Infrastructure:
  Fiscal Policy and Public Spending, Small Business Support, Banking and Financial Services, Trade and Industry

Key Points:
James Murray (Labour (Co-op), Ealing North):
    James Murray explained that from 2026-27, the government plans to introduce permanently lower business rates for high street retail, hospitality, and leisure properties. To fund this, a higher rate will be applied to properties valued over £500,000. The Non-Domestic Rating (Multipliers and Private Schools) Bill will enable these changes.
    

Jonathan Hinder (Labour, Pendle and Clitheroe):
    Jonathan Hinder criticised the previous Conservative government for leaving many shops empty in his constituency, emphasising the value of small businesses to local economies. He asked for assurance that multinational companies will pay their fair share under Labour to support high street growth.
    Context: Responding to James Murray's point on business rates reforms.

James Murray (Labour (Co-op), Ealing North):
    James Murray assured Jonathan Hinder that the reforms include ensuring properties with a rateable value of £500,000 or more contribute to permanent tax cuts for high street businesses, including large distribution warehouses used by online retailers.
    Context: In response to Jonathan Hinder's concerns about multinationals and local businesses.

Connor Naismith (Labour, Crewe and Nantwich):
    Connor Naismith highlighted the importance of business rates reform for small high street businesses, noting feedback from local businesses like St Martha Greek taverna, and asked what steps are being taken to ensure their continued success.
    Context: Addressing James Murray following the mention of business rates reform.

James Murray (Labour (Co-op), Ealing North):
    James Murray congratulated St Martha Greek taverna and noted that the planned business rates reform will support retail, hospitality, and leisure businesses. He expressed optimism that such businesses, including the taverna, would be eligible for the tax cut.
    Context: In response to Connor Naismith's point about support for local businesses.

Jade Botterill (Labour, Ossett and Denby Dale):
    Jade Botterill expressed satisfaction with a new banking hub in Ossett, previously bankless, and inquired about further government support for communities relying on cash and banking services, particularly in rural areas.
    Context: Change of subject to banking services, unrelated to business rates.

James Murray (Labour (Co-op), Ealing North):
    James Murray confirmed plans to roll out 350 banking hubs nationwide, aiming to support local communities by ensuring access to cash and banking services, benefiting areas like Ossett.
    Context: In response to Jade Botterill's inquiry on banking support.

Sammy Wilson (Democratic Unionist Party, East Antrim):
    Sammy Wilson criticised the impact of business rates and national insurance hikes on small businesses, predicting negative outcomes like unemployment, inflation, and potentially higher future taxes.
    

James Murray (Labour (Co-op), Ealing North):
    James Murray responded by explaining the doubling of the employment allowance to help small businesses hire without paying national insurance. He invited further input from Wilson on public services funding derived from such policies.
    Context: Responding to Sammy Wilson's criticism of government taxation policies.

Gideon Amos (Liberal Democrat, Taunton and Wellington):
    Gideon Amos raised concerns about the challenges faced by hospitality businesses like Mr Miles in Taunton due to high taxes and rateable value limits restricting discounts. He suggested reviewing the business rates discount limit of £50,000 for small businesses.
    Context: Questioning the current limits on rateable values affecting tax reliefs.

James Murray (Labour (Co-op), Ealing North):
    James Murray reiterated the government's plan for permanent tax cuts for businesses with rateable values below £500,000, asserting it provides stability and aiding investment and growth. He called for support for these reforms.
    Context: Responding to Gideon Amos's query about adjusting rateable value limits for tax benefits.
